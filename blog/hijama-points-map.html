Hijama Points Map: A Comprehensive Full Body Guide

Understanding hijama points — the specific anatomical locations where cups are applied for therapeutic benefit — is fundamental knowledge for both hijama practitioners and patients seeking to understand their treatment. Traditional Islamic medicine identifies specific points based on prophetic guidance (hadith), classical Tibb Nabawi texts (including Ibn al-Qayyim's Zad al-Ma'ad and al-Suyuti's Tibb al-Nabi), and centuries of clinical observation by Muslim physicians. Modern hijama practice combines these traditional reference points with contemporary anatomical understanding and acupressure/trigger point knowledge.

Key Takeaway: There are over 108 recognised hijama points in the classical Islamic medical tradition, ranging from the prophetically specified points (which have the strongest recommendation) to condition-specific points identified through clinical experience. Every treatment is individualised — a qualified practitioner selects the appropriate combination of points based on the patient's condition, constitution, and presentation.

The Prophetically Specified Points

Al-Kahil (Point 1) — The Master Point: Located at the base of the neck between the shoulder blades, at the C7-T1 junction — the prominent vertebra at the base of the neck. Al-Kahil is the single most important hijama point, described in hadith as the "master point" used by the Prophet ﷺ himself. It is indicated for: general wellness and preventive hijama, headaches and migraines, neck and shoulder pain, neurological conditions, blood pressure regulation, and immune system support. Al-Kahil is included in almost every hijama treatment as a primary or supporting point.

Al-Akhda'ain (Points 2 & 3) — The Two Neck Veins: Located bilaterally on the neck at the posterior sternocleidomastoid region, adjacent to the external jugular veins. Specifically mentioned in multiple hadith as a point the Prophet ﷺ received hijama. Indicated for: migraines and head pain, neck and cervical pain, facial paralysis, ear conditions, dental pain, jaw pain and TMJ disorders, and conditions of the head and face generally. Al-Akhda'ain is the primary point for headache and migraine treatment in Tibb Nabawi.

Al-Yafukh (Point 4) — The Top of the Head: Located at the vertex of the skull (the crown of the head). Mentioned in hadith as a point used by the Prophet ﷺ. Indicated for: severe headaches, hair loss, scalp conditions, and neurological conditions. Applied as dry cupping due to the sensitivity of this area. Requires particular skill and is typically reserved for experienced practitioners.

Al-Warik (Points 5 & 6) — The Hip Points: Located bilaterally over the iliac crest and hip region. Indicated for hip pain, sciatica, lower limb numbness, lumbar disc conditions, and lower back pain.

Back and Spine Points

Paravertebral Points (T1–T12): A series of points on either side of the thoracic spine, approximately 1.5 finger-widths lateral to the spinous processes. These points correspond to specific organ reflex zones and nerve root levels: upper thoracic (T1–T4) for heart and lung conditions; mid-thoracic (T5–T8) for liver, gallbladder, and digestive conditions; lower thoracic (T9–T12) for kidney, adrenal, and small intestine functions. Paravertebral cupping addresses both local spinal conditions and the reflex zones associated with each thoracic level.

Lumbar Points (L1–L5): The lumbar paravertebral points are among the most frequently treated in hijama practice due to the prevalence of lower back pain. These points address: lower back pain, disc herniations, kidney conditions, reproductive health (through the kidney and pelvic organ reflex zones), and lower limb pain and weakness from lumbar nerve root compression.

Sacral Points: The sacrum (the triangular bone at the base of the spine) is a primary treatment zone for: sciatica and piriformis syndrome, pelvic floor conditions, reproductive health (uterus, ovaries, prostate), bladder and bowel conditions, and coccyx pain. Sacral cupping is a cornerstone of the hijama protocol for women's health conditions including PCOS, menstrual disorders, and fertility support.

Head and Face Points

Beyond the prophetically specified head points (Al-Kahil, Al-Akhda'ain, Al-Yafukh), additional head and face points are used for specific conditions: suboccipital points (at the base of the skull) for cervicogenic headache, occipital neuralgia, and visual disturbances; temporal points (at the temples) for temporal headaches, jaw tension, and TMJ disorders; sinus points (alongside the nose and cheekbones) for chronic sinusitis, allergic rhinitis, and sinus headaches; and forehead points for frontal headaches and concentration difficulties.

Abdomen and Chest Points

Abdominal hijama is used for digestive conditions (irritable bowel syndrome, constipation, bloating), liver and gallbladder conditions, reproductive organ health (lower abdomen), and metabolic conditions. The epigastric region (upper central abdomen) addresses stomach and liver function. The umbilical region is indicated for digestive and pelvic conditions. Lower abdominal points are used for reproductive health in both men and women. Chest cupping (anterior and lateral thorax) is used for respiratory conditions including asthma, chronic cough, and bronchitis.

Lower Limb Points

Key lower limb hijama points include: the inner thigh and inner knee (SP9 region — classical reproductive and endocrine point in combined hijama-acupuncture practice), the posterior thigh and hamstring belly (for hamstring tightness, sciatica, and posterior knee pain), the calf (gastrocnemius) for calf pain, peripheral vascular disease, and lower limb oedema, and the Achilles tendon region for Achilles tendinopathy and heel pain.

Frequently Asked Questions: Hijama Points

How does a practitioner decide which hijama points to use?

Point selection in professional hijama practice is based on: the patient's presenting condition and chief complaint, physical examination findings (areas of tenderness, restriction, discolouration), the traditional Tibb Nabawi point indications for the condition, the patient's constitution and overall health status, and contraindications to specific points (e.g., avoiding lower back and abdominal points in pregnancy). A good practitioner takes a thorough history, performs a physical assessment, and selects a personalised combination of 4–8 points appropriate for the individual session.

Is it safe to apply hijama cups anywhere on the body?

No. There are areas where cupping is contraindicated or requires special caution: over bony prominences (spine, sternum, ribs, joints), over major blood vessels and nerves, over the kidneys in wet hijama, over varicose veins, over areas of active skin infection or open wounds, and over tumours or malignancies. The abdomen and lower back require special caution during pregnancy. A qualified practitioner knows these contraindicated areas and positions cups safely relative to them.

What is Al-Kahil and why is it called the master point?

Al-Kahil is located at the C7-T1 junction — the prominent bone at the base of your neck where the neck meets the upper back. It is called the master point because: it is the most frequently mentioned point in prophetic hadith for general therapeutic use, it is anatomically located over the spinal cord and a dense network of autonomic nerve fibres, and its stimulation produces broad systemic effects across multiple organ systems and physiological processes simultaneously. Including Al-Kahil in a hijama session provides a systemic regulatory effect that enhances the benefits of all other points treated in the same session.

Can I request specific hijama points from my practitioner?

Yes. It is entirely appropriate to discuss your condition and treatment goals with your hijama practitioner and to ask about which points are being used and why. A good practitioner welcomes this engagement and will explain the rationale for their point selection. However, the final treatment plan should reflect the practitioner's clinical assessment rather than patient self-prescription — they may identify treatment needs and appropriate points that the patient is not aware of.
