Hijama in Islam: Sunnah Days, Prophetic Guidance & Spiritual Benefits

Hijama occupies a uniquely elevated position in Islamic medical tradition — it is not merely a therapeutic practice endorsed by the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ but a Sunnah muakkadah (confirmed prophetic practice) whose performance carries both physical healing and spiritual reward. For Muslim patients and practitioners, hijama represents the beautiful intersection of prophetic guidance and holistic healing, a practice simultaneously commanded by Allah's Messenger and validated by modern medical science. This comprehensive guide covers the authentic hadith evidence for hijama, the Sunnah days and Islamic lunar calendar recommendations, the principles of Tibb Nabawi, and the spiritual dimensions of this prophetic medicine.

Key Hadith: "The best medicine with which you treat yourselves is hijama (cupping)." — Sahih al-Bukhari 5696, narrated by Ibn Abbas (RA). "Indeed the best of remedies you have is hijama, and if there was something excellent to be used as a remedy then it is hijama." — Sunan Abu Dawud 3857.

Authentic Hadith Evidence for Hijama

The evidential basis for hijama in Islamic medicine comes from numerous authenticated (sahih) hadith collected in the major books of hadith including Sahih al-Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, Sunan Abu Dawud, Jami al-Tirmidhi, and Sunan Ibn Majah. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ both received hijama personally on numerous occasions and recommended it explicitly to his companions and community. Key hadith include:

"The Prophet ﷺ had himself cupped on the two jugular veins and on the top of the head." (Reported by Anas ibn Malik RA, Sunan Abu Dawud 3860)

"Healing is in three things: drinking honey, the knife of the cupper (hijama), and cauterisation with fire. I forbid my nation from cauterisation." (Sahih al-Bukhari 5681)

"The Messenger of Allah ﷺ said: 'Jibril informed me that hijama (cupping) is the most helpful thing for people to use for healing.'" (Sunan Ibn Majah 3476)

"If there is any good in your medicines, then it is in the incision of the cupper, drinking honey, or cauterisation with fire, and I do not like to be cauterised." (Sahih al-Bukhari 5683)

These hadith are not merely incidental references — they represent a sustained prophetic endorsement across multiple occasions, narrators, and collections, establishing hijama as one of the most strongly authenticated practices in the Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ.

Sunnah Days for Hijama: The Islamic Lunar Calendar

The Islamic tradition provides specific guidance on the optimal days of the lunar calendar for hijama, based on hadith guidance about the physiological state of the body during the lunar cycle and the Prophet's own practice. The primary Sunnah days for hijama are the 17th, 19th, and 21st of any month in the Islamic (Hijri) lunar calendar. The hadith supporting this states: "Whoever performs hijama on the 17th, 19th, and 21st day of the month will be cured from every disease." (Sunan Abu Dawud 3861). It is further narrated that the Prophet ﷺ preferred Monday, Tuesday, and Thursday for hijama and avoided Wednesday, Friday, Saturday, and Sunday, associating some of these days with increased risk of certain conditions.

Modern practitioners who follow the Tibb Nabawi tradition schedule their hijama sessions on these recommended days, and patients specifically seek treatment on the Sunnah days for the combined physical and spiritual benefit. Many Islamic medical scholars interpret the lunar calendar recommendations as reflecting genuine physiological patterns related to gravitational tidal effects on body fluids across the lunar cycle — an area of emerging scientific interest in chronobiology.

The most blessed combination is when the Sunnah days (17th, 19th, or 21st of the Islamic month) coincide with Monday or Thursday — these are considered the optimal days for hijama in the Islamic medical tradition.

Tibb Nabawi: The Complete System of Prophetic Medicine

Tibb Nabawi (Prophetic Medicine) refers to the body of medical knowledge derived from the authenticated statements, actions, and approvals of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Ibn al-Qayyim al-Jawziyyah's "Medicine of the Prophet" (Zad al-Ma'ad, 14th century CE) remains the most comprehensive classical compilation of Tibb Nabawi, devoting substantial chapters to hijama, its indications, optimal days, points, and conditions. Within the Tibb Nabawi framework, hijama is described as beneficial for conditions affecting the head and neck, skin diseases, liver conditions, abdominal pain, leg pain, and general detoxification — a range of conditions that modern hijama research has systematically begun to investigate and validate.

Hijama Before Ramadan: A Recommended Sunnah Practice

Many Islamic scholars and Tibb Nabawi practitioners recommend performing hijama in the month of Sha'ban (the month before Ramadan) as preparation for the blessed month of fasting. The logic is practical and spiritual: hijama in Sha'ban removes stagnant blood and metabolic waste, improves circulation and energy levels, and prepares the body for the physical and spiritual rigour of Ramadan fasting. Some scholars also recommend hijama immediately after Eid ul-Fitr as a recovery and renewal practice following the month of Ramadan. Hijama during Ramadan while fasting is a matter of scholarly difference — while some scholars permit it, others advise against it due to the physical stress involved and the potential to break the fast (in certain jurisprudential positions).

Spiritual Benefits and Rewards of Hijama

Beyond the physical therapeutic benefits, Islamic scholars have described spiritual dimensions to hijama practice. Performing a prophetic Sunnah with the intention (niyyah) of following the Prophet ﷺ and seeking the healing that Allah has placed in this practice constitutes an act of worship (ibadah) that carries reward. The act of seeking lawful healing (talab al-shifa' bil-halal) itself reflects tawakkul (trust in Allah) and conformity with the prophetic guidance to use the means He has provided for healing. Patients who approach hijama with this spiritual intention and the recitation of the appropriate du'a (supplication) before and after the session are combining physical treatment with spiritual connection — an integration that defines the holistic vision of health in Islamic medicine.

What are the Sunnah days for hijama in 2025?

Is hijama obligatory in Islam?

Hijama is a confirmed Sunnah (Sunnah muakkadah) — strongly recommended based on prophetic practice and hadith — but it is not obligatory (fard). Performing it with the intention of following the Prophet ﷺ and seeking the healing Allah has placed in it earns reward, but its omission is not sinful. Islamic scholars encourage Muslims to avail themselves of this prophetic treatment, particularly those with health conditions for which hijama is indicated.

Can hijama be done in Ramadan while fasting?

This is a matter of scholarly difference. The Shafi'i, Maliki, and Hanbali majority positions consider hijama during Ramadan while fasting to be valid (does not break the fast). The Hanbali position (and the position supported by a hadith "The cupper and the one being cupped have broken their fast") holds that hijama breaks the fast. Given this scholarly difference, many practitioners and patients choose to perform hijama before sunset (iftar) to avoid any doubt, or to schedule sessions outside Ramadan entirely. Consult a knowledgeable Islamic scholar for guidance based on your madhab (jurisprudential school).

What du'a (prayer) should be recited for hijama?

The Islamic tradition recommends reciting Bismillah (In the name of Allah) before beginning any beneficial action. Before receiving hijama, reciting "Bismillah, wa ala barakati Allah" (In the name of Allah and upon the blessing of Allah) is recommended. After the session, a du'a of shukr (gratitude) is appropriate. Some practitioners recite specific Ruqyah (Quranic recitations) during the session for patients seeking both physical and spiritual healing. The intention (niyyah) of following the Sunnah and seeking Allah's healing through His ordained means is itself a form of du'a.
