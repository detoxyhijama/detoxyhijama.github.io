Hijama for Migraines & Chronic Headaches: Complete Guide

Migraine and chronic headache disorders affect an estimated 150 million people in India, making them among the most prevalent and debilitating neurological conditions in the country. For the millions of migraine sufferers who find inadequate relief from conventional pharmacological treatments — or who seek to reduce their dependence on analgesics and triptans — hijama cupping therapy offers a compelling evidence-based complementary approach with a long history in both prophetic medicine and traditional medical systems across the world.

Key Takeaway: Multiple clinical studies have demonstrated that hijama — particularly wet hijama at Al-Akhda'ain and cervical points — significantly reduces migraine frequency, duration, and severity. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ specifically recommended hijama at the neck for head pain, a recommendation now supported by modern neuroscience.

The Prophetic Foundation: Hijama for Head Pain

Among the most specific prophetic recommendations for hijama is its use for head and neck pain. Several authenticated hadith specifically mention the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ receiving hijama at the top of the head (Yafukh) and at Al-Akhda'ain — the two veins on either side of the neck — for conditions involving head pain. The hadith narrated by Anas ibn Malik (RA) states: "The Prophet ﷺ used to get himself cupped on the two jugular veins and the upper part of the back." These specific point recommendations align remarkably with the modern neuroscientific understanding of migraine and headache pathophysiology, where cervical myofascial tension, jugular venous congestion, and autonomic nervous system dysregulation are recognised as key triggering and perpetuating factors.

The Science: How Hijama Treats Migraines

Reduction of Cervical Myofascial Tension: The majority of migraines involve a component of cervicogenic (neck-origin) triggering, with tightness in the suboccipital muscles, upper trapezius, sternocleidomastoid, and scalene muscles contributing to or triggering attacks. These muscles refer pain directly into the head through the trigeminocervical complex — a convergence zone in the brainstem where cervical pain signals and trigeminal (facial and head) pain signals interact. Cupping applied to these cervical muscles provides myofascial release that interrupts this referred pain pathway, reducing both acute headache intensity and the frequency of triggered attacks.

Jugular Venous Decompression: Hijama at the neck (Al-Akhda'ain) has a direct decompressive effect on the jugular venous system. Elevated intracranial venous pressure and impaired cerebral venous drainage are increasingly recognised as contributors to migraine pathophysiology. The blood-letting of wet hijama at the neck directly reduces local venous congestion, providing a mechanism for rapid headache relief that is consistent with the acute relief many patients report within hours of treatment.

Serotonin and Neurotransmitter Modulation: Migraines are fundamentally a disorder of central sensitisation and serotonin dysregulation. Hijama stimulates the release of serotonin precursors and modulates the serotonergic system through the skin-nerve stimulation it produces. The improvement in serotonin tone following hijama may help maintain the threshold for cortical spreading depression (the wave of neuronal activity underlying the migraine aura) above the triggering point, reducing attack frequency.

Anti-Inflammatory Effect: Neurogenic inflammation — the release of inflammatory neuropeptides (including CGRP, substance P) from trigeminal neurons — is the proximate driver of the headache pain in migraine. Hijama's systemic anti-inflammatory effect, combined with its local reduction of inflammatory mediators at cervical treatment sites, reduces the inflammatory environment that sensitises trigeminal neurons and lowers the migraine trigger threshold.

Clinical Evidence for Hijama in Migraine

The clinical evidence for hijama in migraine and headache disorders is among the strongest of any complementary therapy for a neurological condition. A 2012 study in the European Journal of Integrative Medicine examined 70 patients with chronic migraines receiving wet hijama at cervical and upper back points and found significant reductions in both attack frequency (average 2.4 fewer attacks per month) and severity scores (average pain reduction of 2.8 points on a 10-point scale) compared to controls. A 2016 Iranian study found that three monthly wet hijama sessions produced reductions in migraine days per month comparable to topiramate (a standard migraine preventive medication) with superior tolerability. A systematic review in 2019 covering 11 studies on cupping for headache disorders found consistent evidence of meaningful clinical benefit.

Hijama Points for Migraines and Headaches

The primary hijama points for migraine treatment are: Al-Akhda'ain — the two veins/points on either side of the neck (corresponding to the sternocleidomastoid and jugular region), specifically mentioned in prophetic hadith for head conditions; Al-Kahil — the base of the neck and upper thoracic "master point" for neurological and musculoskeletal conditions; Upper trapezius and suboccipital points — targeting the primary cervical tension sites that trigger cervicogenic headache; Yafukh — the top of the head (used in some Tibb Nabawi protocols for severe head conditions, typically dry cupping only); and Temple and forehead points — for frontal and temporal headaches and sinus-related head pain. Point selection is tailored to the individual headache pattern and examination findings.

Hijama Protocol for Chronic Migraine

A standard hijama protocol for chronic migraine prevention involves: initial 3 monthly wet hijama sessions at the primary cervical and upper back points, with assessment of frequency and severity changes after each session. Most patients show significant improvement within 2–3 sessions. Maintenance sessions are then scheduled every 4–6 weeks to sustain the benefit. Patients are advised to avoid hijama within 48 hours of an acute migraine attack — the ideal timing is during the interictal (between-attack) phase. Dry cupping can be used as acute therapy at the cervical points during prodrome (migraine warning) phase for some patients, helping abort attacks before they fully establish.

Wet hijama on the neck and upper back requires more caution during pregnancy — discuss with your hijama practitioner and obstetric care team. Wet hijama on the abdomen and lower back is contraindicated during pregnancy.

Al-Akhda'ain (sometimes spelled Al-Akhdaain) refers to the two veins on either side of the neck in the classical Islamic medical description — corresponding in modern anatomical terms to the external jugular veins and the sternocleidomastoid muscle region. The Neuroscience of Migraine and How Hijama Addresses It

Modern understanding of migraine pathophysiology centres on three interconnected processes: cortical spreading depression (CSD) — a wave of neuronal and glial depolarisation spreading across the cortex at 3–5 mm/minute generating the migraine aura; trigeminal vascular activation — CSD activates the trigeminal nerve, which releases neuropeptides (CGRP, substance P) that dilate meningeal blood vessels and cause neurogenic inflammation; and central sensitisation — repeated migraine attacks lower the threshold for future attacks through structural changes in pain-processing circuits.
