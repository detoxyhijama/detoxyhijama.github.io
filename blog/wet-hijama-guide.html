How to Perform Wet Hijama: A Step-by-Step Practitioner Guide

Wet hijama (Al-Hijamah Al-Damiyah) is the classical Sunnah method of cupping therapy involving both suction and therapeutic blood-letting. It is the most therapeutically powerful form of hijama and the form specifically described in the prophetic hadith. Performing wet hijama correctly requires proper training, the right equipment, strict sterile technique, and a thorough understanding of both the procedure itself and the patient assessment required before treatment. This comprehensive step-by-step guide is intended for trained hijama practitioners as a reference resource and for patients seeking to understand the procedure they will receive.

Safety Notice: Wet hijama should only be performed by trained, qualified practitioners. This guide is for educational purposes and professional reference. Attempting wet hijama without proper training increases infection risk and may cause harm. Enrol in a qualified hijama training course before practising on patients.

Phase 1: Patient Assessment and Preparation

Health History: Before every session, take or review a health history covering: current medications (especially anticoagulants, antiplatelets, and diabetes medications), known bleeding disorders or clotting conditions, active skin conditions at potential treatment sites, pregnancy status, recent surgery or significant illness, current blood pressure, and previous hijama experience and responses. This screening identifies contraindications and guides treatment modifications.

Informed Consent: Explain the procedure fully to the patient including the three-phase process, what they will feel during the incision phase, the cupping marks they can expect, and the aftercare requirements. Obtain verbal (and ideally written for first-time patients) informed consent before proceeding. Answer all patient questions before beginning.

Treatment Point Selection: Based on the health history and physical assessment, select the appropriate hijama points for the session. For a first wet hijama session, conservative point selection (2–4 points at the primary sites for the condition) is recommended rather than comprehensive multi-point treatment. The patient's body can be assessed for point tenderness and resistance as additional guidance for point selection.

Patient Positioning: Position the patient comfortably in a prone (face down) position for back points, seated for neck and head points, or supine for abdominal and anterior points. Use a clean, covered treatment couch. The patient should be warm and relaxed before beginning.

Phase 2: Equipment Preparation

Prepare all necessary equipment before touching the patient: clean, disinfected cups in the appropriate sizes; sterile single-use lancet heads loaded into the cleaned lancet pen (or sterile single-use surgical blades in a blade holder); a new pair of medical-grade latex examination gloves; antiseptic solution (chlorhexidine or povidone-iodine) and sterile cotton for skin preparation; wound dressings or sterile gauze for post-procedure care; and a rigid sharps container within reach for immediate disposal of used lancet heads and blades. Arrange all equipment within easy reach before beginning. Do not begin the procedure until all equipment is ready.

Phase 3: Skin Preparation

Put on the examination gloves. Cleanse the treatment sites thoroughly with antiseptic-soaked sterile cotton — using circular motions from the centre outward to avoid recontaminating the centre. Allow the antiseptic to dry completely (30–60 seconds) before proceeding. Do not blow on the skin to dry it. A second antiseptic wipe after the first has dried provides additional sterility assurance for wet hijama sites.

Phase 4: First Cupping (Dry Phase)

Apply cups to the prepared treatment sites using your chosen method (squeeze-and-release for silicone cups; fire technique for glass cups; pump for vacuum cups). The first cupping phase lasts 3–7 minutes, drawing blood to the skin surface and creating the visible skin elevation inside the cup. Monitor the skin response — a moderate pink-to-red discolouration inside the cup indicates good blood response. Adjust suction if the skin is rising too aggressively (indicating too much suction) or showing minimal response (too little suction). After the appropriate time, remove the cups by releasing the suction slowly.

Phase 5: Incision Phase

This is the most technically demanding phase of wet hijama and the one that most requires hands-on training. With the cups removed, the treatment sites will show raised, hyperaemic (engorged with blood) skin. Using the lancet pen set to the appropriate depth (typically 1.0–1.2mm for standard adult back points), make a series of small, parallel, superficial incisions across the raised skin at each treatment point. The number of incisions per point varies from 5–20 depending on the cup size and point location. Apply consistent, moderate pressure — do not gouge or press hard. The incisions should be just deep enough to penetrate the epidermis into the superficial dermis; they should not be deep cuts. For surgical blade use, the technique involves small, controlled, superficial strokes rather than pushing the blade into the tissue.

Between treatment points, do not remove your gloves. Dispose of the lancet head (or blade) in the sharps container immediately after each point is incised — never set a used lancet or blade down on the treatment surface.

Phase 6: Second Cupping (Wet Phase)

Re-apply the cups over the incision sites. The negative pressure now draws blood through the incisions and into the cup. This is the active blood-letting phase. The second cupping typically lasts 5–10 minutes. Monitor the blood volume accumulating in the cup — the colour, volume, and viscosity of the blood are all clinically informative. Most practitioners remove the cups when the blood flow rate slows significantly (indicating the treatment point has been adequately drained) or at the predetermined time, whichever comes first.

For multi-point treatments, either work through points sequentially (completing both cupping phases at one point before moving to the next) or apply all cups simultaneously after making all incisions. The sequential approach is safer and more controllable for practitioners still developing their technique.

Phase 7: Post-Procedure Care

Remove the cups, wipe away any surface blood with sterile cotton, and apply fresh antiseptic to all incision sites. Assess each site for adequate haemostasis (bleeding has slowed to a negligible rate). Apply wound dressings or sterile gauze padding to each site and secure appropriately. The dressings protect the sites from contamination and absorb any residual ooze in the hours following treatment. Provide the patient with clear verbal and (ideally) written aftercare instructions. Clean and disinfect all equipment immediately after the session, dispose of all single-use items in the appropriate waste streams, and document the session in the patient record.

Frequently Asked Questions: Performing Wet Hijama

How deep should hijama incisions be?

The incisions for wet hijama should be very superficial — penetrating through the epidermis into the upper dermis only, typically 0.8–1.2mm depth for most adult patients. The goal is to create small channels through which blood can be drawn by the cup's suction, not deep cuts. The lancet pen's depth settings are calibrated for this purpose — standard adult setting is 1.0–1.2mm for back and shoulder points, 0.8mm for areas with thinner skin (neck, face, elderly patients). The incisions should not bleed profusely in the absence of cup suction.

How much blood should be drawn in wet hijama?

The volume of blood extracted varies significantly between patients, treatment points, and sessions. A typical single-point session might draw 5–30ml of blood per cup over the 5–10 minute second cupping phase. Total blood volume across all treatment points in a comprehensive session rarely exceeds 100–150ml — well within the physiologically safe limit for a healthy adult. The blood volume drawn should be monitored by the practitioner — if bleeding is profuse or not slowing, suction should be released and direct pressure applied.

Can I perform wet hijama on myself?

No. Wet hijama requires sterile technique, visual access to the treatment sites (typically the back), a second pair of hands, and the ability to respond to any complications. Self-administration of wet hijama is not safe and not recommended under any circumstances. Dry cupping with silicone cups can be self-administered to accessible areas, but wet hijama is strictly a procedure for qualified practitioners treating patients in a clinical setting.

What training is needed to perform wet hijama professionally?

Professional wet hijama practice requires a structured training programme covering anatomy, physiology, the hijama procedure (theory and hands-on practice), sterile technique and infection control, patient assessment and contraindication screening, emergency management, and Islamic medical principles (for Tibb Nabawi practitioners). Look for courses offering at least 2–3 days of intensive training including supervised clinical practice. Several accredited hijama academies operate in India — particularly in Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Hyderabad — offering quality training programmes for new practitioners.

Master-Level Wet Hijama Technique: Advanced Practitioner Guide

This advanced section extends the step-by-step procedure guide with master-level technique refinements, clinical decision-making frameworks, and the practical wisdom that distinguishes excellent hijama practice from merely adequate technique.

Reading the Blood: Advanced Clinical Assessment During Wet Hijama

An experienced hijama practitioner reads the blood drawn during wet hijama as a clinical diagnostic tool. The colour of the extracted blood provides information: bright red blood indicates healthy, well-oxygenated capillary blood and suggests the treatment point is functioning well; darker, more viscous blood (appearing brown-black or very dark red) suggests significant local stagnation and metabolic accumulation — typical in chronic pain areas and areas with long-standing functional impairment; thin, watery blood with poor colour may indicate anaemia or dehydration; the presence of gelatinous or fibrous material in some patients' extracted blood is noted in traditional Islamic medicine literature as a sign of significant stagnation and may be associated with greater therapeutic benefit from the session. The volume of blood extracted: very small volumes despite good suction at wet hijama sites may indicate highly congested tissue with poor capillary permeability; normal therapeutic volumes (10–25ml per cup over 5–10 minutes) indicate appropriate tissue response; very large rapid volumes may indicate excessive incision depth or a patient with anticoagulant medication not disclosed in the health history.

Multi-Point Wet Hijama: Advanced Simultaneous Protocol

Advanced wet hijama practitioners develop the technique of multi-point simultaneous wet hijama — performing incisions at multiple treatment sites and applying cups to all sites simultaneously rather than sequentially. This approach is appropriate for comprehensive sessions covering 4–8 points and provides: simultaneous therapeutic effect across multiple tissue regions; more efficient use of session time for both practitioner and patient; and the systemic synergistic effect of simultaneous multi-site blood-letting that single-point sequential treatment cannot provide. The technical requirements for safe simultaneous multi-point wet hijama are: complete preparation of all cups and sterile materials before beginning; methodical, systematic incision of all sites before placing any cups (never leave a patient with blood flowing from unprotected incision sites while attending to other sites); working in pairs with an assistant for large multi-point sessions; and having a clear mental map of incision sites, their relative depth requirements, and expected blood volumes before beginning.

Extended Frequently Asked Questions

How deep should hijama incisions be for different body areas?

Incision depth varies by body area and patient factors: standard adult back and shoulder areas: 1.0–1.2mm; neck region: 0.8–1.0mm; areas with thinner skin (elderly patients, forearms, calves): 0.8mm; areas with very thick skin or significant subcutaneous fat (central lower back in obese patients): 1.2–1.5mm; facial hijama (rare, advanced only): 0.6–0.8mm; paediatric patients: 0.8mm maximum. These settings assume a standard auto-lancet pen with calibrated depth control. For surgical blades, depth control depends entirely on practitioner technique — the blade should be used with the minimum force required to produce superficial skin separation, never pressed into the tissue.

What is the correct suction pressure for wet hijama?

The therapeutic suction pressure for wet hijama ranges from approximately −200 to −400 millibars negative pressure for the initial dry cupping phase (bringing blood to the surface) and may be maintained at this level or slightly reduced for the second (post-incision) cupping phase. In practical terms for silicone cups: squeeze the cup to approximately 40–60% of its resting diameter before placement for standard adult back treatment; reduce to 30–40% compression for the neck, elderly patients, and sensitive areas. For vacuum pump cups, start at the lowest effective setting and increase to patient tolerance. The patient should feel firm suction but should not feel acute pain from the cup pressure alone — a sharp, stabbing pain from the cup pressure (rather than the incision) suggests excessive suction.

How do you know when to remove the cups in wet hijama?

The primary indicators for cup removal in the wet phase: the rate of blood flow into the cup has decreased markedly from its initial rate (indicating the point has been adequately drained for this session); the cup has been in place for the standard treatment time (5–8 minutes for most back points); the patient reports increasing discomfort from the cup (beyond the expected therapeutic sensation); or the cup has filled to 70–80% capacity (to prevent blood overflow and maintain suction). In clinical practice, the combination of time elapsed and visual assessment of blood flow rate is the most reliable guide.

Can the same hijama points be treated every session?

It is generally advisable to vary the precise incision locations slightly within a treatment zone across successive sessions rather than incising exactly the same skin coordinates repeatedly. This reduces the cumulative scar tissue risk and allows different capillary territory within the point to be accessed. The treatment zone (e.g., the Al-Kahil area at C7-T1) remains consistent across sessions, but the specific incision sites within that zone are slightly varied. This is an important technique refinement that distinguishes experienced practitioners from newer ones.

What is the maximum number of wet hijama sessions per month?

For healthy adults with no significant health conditions, wet hijama can theoretically be performed monthly (every 3–4 weeks) as a standard maintenance schedule. The traditional guidance is to allow the body's haemostatic and regenerative response to fully complete before repeating — typically 3–4 weeks. More frequent wet hijama (every 2 weeks) may be appropriate for specific acute conditions under experienced practitioner guidance. More frequent than every 2 weeks is generally not recommended as it may not allow sufficient time for the full therapeutic and regenerative response from each session to develop.
