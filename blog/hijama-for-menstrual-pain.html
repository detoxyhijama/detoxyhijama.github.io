Point Palpation Technique for Hijama For Menstrual Pain

Before applying cups, experienced practitioners palpate the planned treatment points to identify areas of maximum tenderness, tissue resistance, and stagnation — information that guides final point selection and suction intensity. For menstrual pain, the palpation technique involves firm but gentle circular pressure at the planned treatment sites, with the patient reporting any points of particularly sharp or radiating tenderness. Suction Mechanics for Hijama For Menstrual Pain: Technical Guide

The therapeutic suction level for menstrual pain conditions falls in the range of approximately 200–400 millibars negative pressure for most adult patients. In practical terms for different cup types: silicone cups should be compressed to 40–60% of their resting cup diameter before placement on most adult back and body areas; facial cups require only 20–30% compression; electric devices should be set to levels 4–6 of a 9-level scale for standard treatment. The correct suction level is sufficient to create visible skin elevation within the cup (the cup should fill to 1/3–1/2 its depth with elevated skin) without causing acute pain at the cup edge. Patients with menstrual pain may require slight suction adjustment based on: local skin sensitivity in the affected area, age-related skin changes in elderly patients, medication effects on skin fragility, and the specific tissue type at each treatment point.

Wet Hijama Incision Technique for menstrual pain

The incision phase of wet hijama for menstrual pain requires precise technique to achieve the therapeutic blood extraction while minimising unnecessary tissue trauma. Key technical points: set the lancet pen depth to 1.0–1.2mm for standard adult back and body points (reduce to 0.8mm for neck, elderly patients, and areas with thinner skin); apply the lancet pen perpendicular to the skin surface with consistent moderate pressure — never angle the device or apply excessive force; make 8–20 small incisions per treatment point depending on cup size, distributing them evenly across the elevated skin to allow uniform blood extraction; work methodically through all planned incision sites before placing the blood-letting cups, maintaining full awareness of all incised sites to prevent inadvertent blood contamination of the treatment field; and dispose of each lancet head immediately after use in the dedicated sharps container — never set down a used lancet on any surface.

Reading Treatment Response During Hijama For Menstrual Pain

An experienced hijama practitioner reads multiple real-time indicators during a Hijama For Menstrual Pain session to assess therapeutic response and guide technique adjustment: the rate and volume of blood extraction in the wet phase (normal: steady flow reducing over 5–10 minutes; concerning: excessive rapid flow or complete absence of flow); the colour and viscosity of extracted blood (dark, viscous blood indicates significant stagnation; bright red thin blood indicates healthy circulation; the balance between these reflects tissue status); the pattern of post-session cupping marks (intensity, distribution, and symmetry all provide diagnostic information about tissue status); the patient's reported sensations (therapeutic sensation of pressure and warmth vs acute pain requiring suction reduction); and the patient's autonomic response (flushing, pallor, sweating, heart rate changes observed as signs of vasovagal response requiring immediate position change and session pause).
