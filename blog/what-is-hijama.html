What is Hijama? Understanding Al-Hijamah Cupping Therapy

Hijama, also known as Al-Hijamah in Arabic, is one of the oldest and most effective forms of therapeutic medicine practised across civilisations for thousands of years. The word "hijama" derives from the Arabic root "hajama," meaning to suck or to reduce in size — a direct reference to the fundamental mechanism by which the therapy operates. In its most complete form, hijama combines the application of suction cups to the skin with controlled superficial incisions to draw out stagnant blood and metabolic waste products from the body's subcutaneous tissues.

In the Islamic medical tradition, hijama holds a unique and elevated status. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ not only practised and recommended hijama personally but also described it in authenticated hadith as one of the best forms of treatment available to humanity. This prophetic endorsement — combined with the growing body of modern scientific evidence supporting cupping therapy's physiological benefits — has led to an extraordinary resurgence of hijama practice across India and the Muslim world in recent decades.

Prophetic Hadith on Hijama: "The best medicine with which you treat yourselves is hijama." — Sahih al-Bukhari 5696, narrated by Ibn 'Abbas (RA).

Types of Hijama (Cupping Therapy)

Understanding the different forms of hijama is essential before beginning any therapeutic programme. There are three primary categories, each with distinct mechanisms, applications, and appropriate equipment requirements.

1. Dry Cupping (Al-Hijamah Al-Jaffah)

Dry cupping involves placing cups on the skin and creating suction without any incision. The negative pressure created inside the cup causes the skin and superficial muscle layers to be drawn upward into the cup. This mechanical decompression has several documented physiological effects: it increases local blood circulation, stimulates the nervous system through skin mechanoreceptors, promotes lymphatic drainage, and creates a "reverse pressure" effect that stretches the myofascial tissue beneath the skin. Dry cupping is suitable for beginners, for patients who are contraindicated for wet methods, and for general wellness and relaxation purposes. Equipment needed: silicone cups, glass cups, or a vacuum pump kit.

2. Wet Hijama — Al-Hijamah Al-Damiyah (Wet Cupping)

Wet hijama is the classical Sunnah method and involves three steps: initial dry cupping to bring blood to the surface, controlled superficial incisions using a sterile lancet or surgical blade, and a second round of cupping to draw out the blood through the incisions. This is the form specifically referenced in prophetic medicine and the type most commonly practised at hijama clinics across India. The blood drawn out is considered "stagnant blood" (dam fasid in Arabic) — blood that has accumulated in the superficial capillary beds and is believed in traditional medicine to carry metabolic waste, inflammatory mediators, and toxins. Modern research has found that blood extracted through wet hijama contains higher concentrations of inflammatory markers than venous blood, providing scientific support for the traditional understanding.

3. Moving Cupping (Al-Hijamah Al-Mutaharrikah)

Moving cupping — also known as gliding cupping or massage cupping — involves applying oil to the skin and then moving a cup in sliding motions across the treatment area rather than keeping it stationary. This technique provides a deep myofascial release effect, stretching the fascia longitudinally and releasing adhesions between tissue layers. It is particularly valuable for sports recovery, back and neck stiffness, IT band syndrome, and as a preparatory technique before wet hijama.

The Science Behind Hijama: How It Works

Modern biomedical research has significantly advanced our understanding of the physiological mechanisms underlying hijama therapy. Several well-documented mechanisms have been identified:

Increased Local Blood Circulation: The negative pressure created by cupping causes significant vasodilation in the tissue beneath the cup. Nitric oxide release from endothelial cells in response to mechanical stimulation drives this vasodilation. The resulting increase in blood flow delivers oxygen, nutrients, and immune cells to the treated area while simultaneously accelerating the removal of metabolic waste products.

Stimulation of the Lymphatic System: Cupping therapy has been demonstrated to stimulate lymphatic flow, enhancing the drainage of interstitial fluid and the clearance of inflammatory mediators from tissues. This is particularly relevant in conditions involving chronic inflammation and oedema.

Neuromodulation: Cutaneous nerve stimulation by the suction cups activates multiple neural pathways including the autonomic nervous system. Research has demonstrated that hijama can modulate the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, reducing cortisol levels and activating the parasympathetic nervous system — effects that explain the profound relaxation and stress relief commonly reported after hijama sessions.

Release of Inflammatory Mediators: Wet hijama specifically induces the release of analgesic compounds including beta-endorphins and enkephalins, prostaglandin E1, serotonin precursors, and anti-inflammatory cytokines. The mechanical stimulation of mast cells in the skin dermis during cupping triggers localised release of histamine, which paradoxically has anti-inflammatory effects at physiological doses by upregulating the body's anti-inflammatory response.

Hijama in Islamic Medicine — Tibb Nabawi

Tibb Nabawi (Prophetic Medicine) is the body of medical knowledge derived from the authenticated statements and practices of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Within this tradition, hijama occupies a position of supreme importance. Multiple sahih (authentic) hadith describe the Prophet himself receiving hijama, recommending it to his companions, and indicating specific days on the Islamic lunar calendar when its benefits are maximised.

The most frequently cited hadith regarding hijama days refers to the 17th, 19th, and 21st of the Islamic lunar month — particularly when these days fall on a Monday or Thursday. These are considered the Sunnah days for hijama. Contemporary practitioners of Tibb Nabawi schedule their sessions around the lunar calendar, and many patients specifically request the Sunnah days for their hijama treatments.

Safety and Contraindications

While hijama is generally safe when performed correctly by a trained practitioner, certain contraindications must be respected. Hijama should not be performed on: patients taking blood-thinning medication (anticoagulants), patients with haemophilia or other bleeding disorders, pregnant women (particularly over the abdomen and lower back), patients with active skin infections or open wounds at the treatment site, patients with active cancer or undergoing chemotherapy, those with severe anaemia, and patients who have eaten a heavy meal within 2 hours.

Always consult a qualified hijama practitioner and, where relevant, a medical doctor before beginning hijama treatment for any medical condition.

The Etymology and Historical Roots of Hijama

The Arabic word "hijama" (حجامة) derives from the root verb "hajama" (حَجَمَ), meaning to suck, to draw out, or to reduce. Related Arabic terms include "hajim" (the practitioner who performs hijama), "mihjam" (the cupping instrument), and "dam al-fasid" (the stagnant or corrupted blood removed during wet cupping). In classical Arabic medical literature, hijama is categorised under the broader discipline of Al-Tibb Al-Nabawi (Prophetic Medicine) and Al-Tibb Al-Islami (Islamic Medicine). The synonymous terms used across historical and contemporary sources include: Al-Hijamah Al-Damiyah (wet cupping), Al-Hijamah Al-Jaffah (dry cupping), Al-Hijamah Al-Mutaharrikah (moving cupping), fasd (phlebotomy — a related but distinct bloodletting technique), and in non-Arabic traditions: cupping therapy, suction cup therapy, vacuum cupping, and therapeutic cupping.

The historical record of cupping therapy extends across virtually every major civilisation in recorded history. In ancient Egypt, the Ebers Papyrus (c. 1550 BCE) documents cupping therapy. In ancient Greece, Hippocrates (460–370 BCE) wrote extensively on cupping, describing its applications for conditions including pneumonia, menstrual disorders, and musculoskeletal pain — 2,400 years before modern randomised controlled trials would confirm many of his clinical observations. In traditional Chinese medicine, cupping (拔罐, bá guàn) has been practised for over 2,000 years and remains an integral component of TCM practice. In the Islamic golden age (8th–13th centuries CE), scholars including Ibn Sina (Avicenna), Ibn al-Qayyim al-Jawziyyah, and al-Suyuti systematically documented hijama's applications, indications, contraindications, and technique in authoritative medical texts that remain referenced today.

The Three Pillars of Hijama's Mechanism of Action

Pillar 1 — Mechanical (Physical) Effects: The physical suction of the cup creates a pressure differential of typically −300 to −500 millibars below atmospheric pressure. This negative pressure: lifts the skin and subcutaneous tissue away from underlying structures; stretches the dermal and subcutaneous connective tissue (fascia) vertically and radially; dilates superficial capillaries and venules through mechanical stress on vessel walls; activates skin mechanoreceptors (Meissner's corpuscles, Merkel's discs, Ruffini endings, Pacinian corpuscles) that feed into the central nervous system; and creates a pressure gradient that mobilises interstitial fluid toward lymphatic drainage vessels. The specific magnitude and duration of suction determines the depth and character of the tissue response — ranging from gentle lymphatic stimulation at minimal suction to significant myofascial release and deep tissue mobilisation at therapeutic suction levels.

Pillar 2 — Neurological Effects: The skin's dense sensory innervation makes it a uniquely powerful portal for neurological intervention. Cupping activates multiple afferent (sensory) nerve fibre types: A-beta fibres (large, myelinated, fast-conducting) transmitting tactile and pressure information that engages the gate-control mechanism of pain inhibition in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord; C-fibres (small, unmyelinated, slow-conducting) that modulate autonomic function and trigger neuropeptide release including substance P, CGRP, and vasoactive intestinal peptide; and sympathetic postganglionic fibres that regulate local vascular and immune responses. Through these neural pathways, cupping at peripheral skin sites can influence central nervous system function at a distance, explaining the systemic effects (improved mood, reduced cortisol, better sleep) that occur with hijama even when treatment is applied only to the back.

Pillar 3 — Biochemical Effects: The tissue trauma and inflammatory response induced by cupping triggers a precisely orchestrated biochemical cascade: mast cell degranulation releasing histamine, heparin, serotonin, and chemokines; prostaglandin production driving local vasodilation and increased vascular permeability; cytokine release including IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, and the anti-inflammatory IL-10; endogenous opioid release (beta-endorphins, enkephalins, dynorphins) providing natural analgesia; activation of the complement system; and stimulation of the nitric oxide signalling pathway driving sustained vasodilation. These biochemical changes collectively constitute a "controlled, therapeutic inflammation" — using the body's own healing mechanisms to produce therapeutic benefit.

Hijama vs Other Therapeutic Modalities: Comprehensive Comparison

Hijama vs Acupuncture: Both modalities work through skin stimulation to produce neurological and physiological effects, but through different mechanisms. Acupuncture inserts fine needles to specific depths to stimulate Qi flow and acupuncture points; hijama applies suction to the skin surface over a broader area. Hijama's unique advantage is the blood-letting component of wet hijama, which removes specific blood-borne inflammatory mediators that acupuncture cannot address. Acupuncture's advantage is its precision at specific point depths. Many integrative practitioners combine both modalities. In clinical trials, both have demonstrated efficacy for pain and musculoskeletal conditions, with hijama showing particularly strong evidence for conditions with a significant vascular or inflammatory component.

Hijama vs Deep Tissue Massage: Both address myofascial restriction and improve local circulation, but through opposite pressure vectors. Massage compresses tissue (positive pressure); cupping decompresses tissue (negative pressure). Cupping's negative pressure lifts the skin away from underlying structures, creating a "reverse stretch" that is simply not achievable through conventional massage. This makes cupping uniquely effective for myofascial adhesions where compression would be counterproductive. Cupping also reaches deeper tissue layers with less force than would be required through massage — important for elderly patients, post-surgical tissues, and areas with significant tenderness.

Hijama vs Physiotherapy: Physiotherapy targets rehabilitation through exercise, movement re-education, and manual therapy. Hijama excels at rapid reduction of inflammation and myofascial release that then enables physiotherapy exercises to be performed more effectively. The two modalities work synergistically — hijama preparing the tissue for rehabilitation; physiotherapy consolidating the gains through functional strengthening and movement correction.

Hijama vs NSAID Medication: NSAIDs (ibuprofen, diclofenac, naproxen) reduce inflammation through COX enzyme inhibition. Hijama reduces inflammation through physical removal of inflammatory mediators and the body's own anti-inflammatory response. NSAIDs are faster-acting for acute inflammation but carry significant gastrointestinal, cardiovascular, and renal risks with chronic use. Hijama's effects develop over hours to days but persist for weeks without organ toxicity risk. For patients with chronic pain requiring long-term anti-inflammatory treatment, hijama offers a meaningful risk-benefit advantage.

Understanding Cupping Marks: The Complete Guide

The circular discolouration marks that appear after hijama are one of the most commonly misunderstood aspects of the therapy, frequently described incorrectly as "bruises." Understanding what these marks actually represent is important for both practitioners and patients.

What causes cupping marks? The negative pressure inside the cup causes extravasation — the movement of red blood cells from capillaries into the surrounding subcutaneous tissue. This is different from conventional bruising (which results from traumatic impact rupturing vessels) in that it involves controlled, non-traumatic extravasation through the intact vessel wall under the influence of pressure gradient.

Why do different areas produce different colour marks? The colour intensity reflects the local concentration of extravasated blood, which in turn reflects the degree of local microcirculatory stagnation and tissue congestion. Areas with significant metabolic stagnation (often associated with chronic pain, overused muscles, or poor circulation) produce darker, more intense marks. Healthy, well-circulated tissue produces lighter marks. This diagnostic dimension of cupping marks has been used in traditional medicine for centuries — a practitioner observing the pattern and intensity of marks gains information about which body areas have the most significant therapeutic need.

How long do marks last? The extravasated red blood cells are progressively broken down by tissue macrophages (the immune cells responsible for clearing cellular debris) over a period of 3–10 days, depending on the mark intensity and the individual's metabolic rate. Lighter marks clear in 3–5 days; darker marks may take 7–10 days. The breakdown of haemoglobin produces the same colour progression seen in conventional bruises: from red/purple initially to blue/green to yellow/brown as haemoglobin is metabolised to biliverdin and bilirubin. Marks are completely harmless and no specific treatment is required for their resolution.

Progressive lightening of marks: An important clinical observation consistently reported by experienced hijama practitioners is that marks at the same anatomical locations progressively lighten across successive treatment sessions. This reflects the actual improvement in local tissue circulation and reduction in stagnation — one of the clearest objective indicators that the treatment is producing the intended physiological changes.

Extended FAQ: What Is Hijama — 50 Most Searched Questions

Is hijama the same as cupping therapy?

Hijama and cupping therapy refer to the same core technique — applying suction cups to the skin — but hijama specifically refers to the Islamic prophetic medical tradition of Al-Hijamah, which includes both dry and wet cupping and carries spiritual significance in Islamic medicine. "Cupping therapy" is the broader Western clinical term encompassing all cup-based negative-pressure therapies including traditional Chinese cupping, fire cupping, vacuum cupping, and hijama. When people in India and the Muslim world say "hijama," they typically mean the complete practice including its Islamic context; "cupping therapy" is the medically neutral equivalent term.

How long has hijama been practised?

Cupping therapy has been documented in human medical practice for at least 3,500 years, with references in the ancient Egyptian Ebers Papyrus dating to approximately 1550 BCE. In the Islamic tradition, hijama received its definitive religious endorsement through the authenticated hadith of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in the 7th century CE, establishing it as a formally recommended therapeutic practice within Islamic medicine. The unbroken tradition of hijama practice from the time of the Prophet to the present day represents one of the longest continuous therapeutic traditions in human history.

What does a hijama session feel like?

Most patients describe the sensation during dry cupping as a firm pulling or drawing sensation — similar to a deep suction massage, but in the reverse direction. The sensation becomes more pronounced over areas with significant muscle tension or metabolic stagnation. During wet hijama, the incision phase adds a brief, sharp sensation lasting under a second — most patients describe it as similar to a small scratch or needle prick. Post-session, many patients feel deeply relaxed, sometimes drowsy, and experience a pleasant warmth and lightness in the treated areas. Mild tenderness at cupping sites is normal for 24–48 hours. Overall, the majority of patients who approach hijama with reasonable expectations report the experience as significantly more comfortable than they anticipated.

How is hijama different from phlebotomy (blood donation)?

Both involve drawing blood, but through fundamentally different mechanisms with different physiological effects. Blood donation (phlebotomy) draws blood from a deep venous access point (typically the antecubital vein in the arm), removing a standard volume (typically 450ml) of whole blood for therapeutic purposes (as in treating haemochromatosis) or for donation. Hijama draws blood through superficial skin incisions from the capillary-rich subcutaneous layer in response to the focused suction of a cup — volumes are much smaller (5–30ml per point), the blood drawn has a different composition from venous blood (higher in inflammatory mediators, ROS, and uric acid), and the therapeutic mechanism is specific to the subcutaneous layer targeted. Phlebotomy addresses systemic blood volume; hijama addresses localised subcutaneous tissue physiology.

What is the best time of day to get hijama?

The classical Islamic medical tradition recommends morning hijama, ideally in the early morning hours before midday. The Prophet ﷺ is reported to have received hijama in the morning. The practical physiological rationale is that cortisol and sympathetic tone are highest in the morning — and hijama's parasympathetic activation and cortisol-lowering effects are most beneficial during the high-cortisol morning period. Modern practitioners generally recommend avoiding hijama within 2 hours of a meal and avoiding late evening sessions (as the deep relaxation response can disrupt sleep for some individuals if the session is too close to bedtime). Morning or early afternoon sessions on an empty or light stomach are generally considered optimal.

How many cups are typically used in a hijama session?

The number of cups applied in a single session varies significantly based on the treatment goal, patient tolerance, and the practitioner's assessment. A focused single-condition treatment might use 2–4 cups at specific points. A comprehensive full-back treatment might use 8–14 cups across multiple paravertebral and specific points. The maximum number of cups appropriate in a single session depends on the patient's overall health, the intensity of wet hijama (blood volume), and post-session recovery capacity. Experienced practitioners err toward fewer, well-placed cups rather than many superficially placed ones — quality of placement and selection trumps quantity.

Can hijama be performed at specific Islamic calendar dates only?

The Sunnah days (17th, 19th, 21st of the Islamic lunar month, preferably on Monday or Thursday) are the traditional preferred days in Tibb Nabawi, but hijama is not restricted to these dates. Many practising Muslims schedule sessions on Sunnah days for the combined spiritual and physical benefit. However, clinical hijama can be performed on any day of the week or lunar month based on the patient's needs — the Sunnah day recommendation represents an optimised schedule based on prophetic guidance, not a restriction on when treatment is valid. For patients with acute conditions requiring timely treatment, waiting for a Sunnah day is not necessary.

All four major Islamic jurisprudence schools (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali) agree on its permissibility and recommendation. Performing hijama with the sincere intention of following the prophetic Sunnah earns spiritual reward alongside its physical benefit.

Can women get hijama?

Yes. Women can and should receive hijama when indicated. Female patients should preferably receive treatment from a female practitioner for modesty. Special considerations apply during menstruation (avoid during active menstrual flow), pregnancy (restricted technique and points), and the postpartum period (40-day nifas waiting period in Islamic tradition before resuming wet hijama).

What are the Sunnah days for hijama?

The prophetically recommended days for hijama are the 17th, 19th, and 21st of the Islamic lunar (Hijri) calendar month, with Monday and Thursday specifically mentioned as additional recommendations. These dates are derived from authenticated hadith. Calculate the corresponding Gregorian dates using an Islamic calendar app. Performing hijama on Sunnah days is spiritually recommended but not mandatory — treatment need not be delayed for these dates when acute conditions require timely intervention.

What is dam fasid in hijama?

Dam fasid (corrupted or stagnant blood) is the classical Islamic medical concept describing blood that has accumulated in superficial capillaries carrying metabolic waste, inflammatory mediators, and toxins. Modern laboratory analysis confirms this concept: wet hijama-extracted blood shows significantly elevated concentrations of uric acid (40–60% higher than venous blood), creatinine (20–30% higher), reactive oxygen species (2–4× higher), and inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α significantly elevated), validating this 1,400-year-old clinical observation with molecular evidence.

Can hijama be done during Ramadan?

This is a matter of scholarly difference. The majority Shafi'i and Maliki positions hold that hijama does not break the fast based on their interpretation of the relevant hadith. The Hanbali position holds that it does break the fast. Many Muslims therefore schedule hijama after iftar (breaking the fast) during Ramadan to avoid any doubt. Consulting a qualified Islamic scholar from your madhab is recommended for a ruling specific to your jurisprudential school.

Black seed oil (Nigella sativa) is the traditional Tibb Nabawi choice — it provides therapeutic benefit alongside the cupping's mechanical effects. How is hijama different from phlebotomy?

Both involve drawing blood but through fundamentally different mechanisms producing different therapeutic effects. Blood donation (phlebotomy) draws blood from a deep venous access point (antecubital vein), removing a standard volume (450ml) of whole blood with haematological composition. Hijama draws blood through superficial skin incisions from the capillary-rich subcutaneous layer in response to focused cup suction — volumes are much smaller (5–30ml per point), the blood drawn has a different and more inflammatory composition than venous blood, and the therapeutic mechanism is specific to the subcutaneous tissue microenvironment rather than systemic blood volume reduction.
